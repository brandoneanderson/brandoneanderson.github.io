Carls Eats App Prototype - Part 1

  • Brandon Ekow Anderson, Zubeyda Shute, Scott Gevurtz, Chloe Simanek
  • App Design

Project idea developed as part of the Human Computer Interactions class.
Carleton College © Winter 2025

Problem Statement

Many Carleton College students often express dissatisfaction with the taste, quality, and variety of dining hall food. This has resulted in decreased meal plan usage, increased food waste, and students seeking alternative dining options. To address this, we developed a prototype for Carls Eats, a food rating app that allows students to easily capture images of their meals, rate dishes, and share captions about dining hall food with their peers. By encouraging frequent ratings and feedback, we aim to elicit improvements in food quality while also providing a space for students to express their opinions and discover new meal options.

Low-Fidelity Prototype

My initial prototype directly addresses the core problem outlined earlier. With the camera feature, students can easily take a photo of their meal or upload an existing image, then add ratings and captions before sharing it on the platform. I’ve also incorporated a dedicated Menu Page, as shown in this figure, which displays a list of current dining meals for that day, the meal’s description, an image, and a filter button which I wanted to expand later on. I also imagined a home page and a personal account page that could additionally tailor the users experience based on their preferences.



Team's UX Research Process

Before I moved forward with improving the prototype, my team first needed to gather insights from fellow students on campus. We dedicated a week to developing questions for a semi-structured interview protocol and a participatory design activity, We initially tested both in class with fellow students and made adjustments based on their feedback. Once the protocols were finalized, each team member conducted interviews with selected students across campus, ensuring we had their consent to record and use the interviews for our research. We then used Otter.ai to transcribe the recordings, which made it easier to conduct axial coding—something I’ll discuss next.

Team's UX Analysis Process

After generating transcripts of our recorded interviews, we reviewed each other’s transcriptions and annotated key excerpts or phrases that related to specific elements of our project—such as phenomena, conditions (causal, contextual, or intervening), actions, and consequences—using a method called axial coding. We then compiled all our annotations and organized them in Figma Jam using sticky notes, a process known as Affinity Diagramming ( Here is the link to the Affinity Diagram ). From this, we developed a codebook, which was crucial for aligning our interpretations of the axial coding process and ensuring consistency across our team’s analysis. To finalize the codebook, we reached a consensus on the codes and then reviewed and refined the axial codes again—this time working in pairs to code each other’s interviews (excluding our own) for added reliability.

Final Team Requirements (Top 3)

Using the finalized codes, we extracted the most critical user needs and translated them into prioritized design requirements for our prototype.

1. Share Your Dining Hall Experience
Students can write and post thoughtful reviews about campus dining hall meals—whether it’s raving about a delicious dish or giving honest feedback about a less-than-stellar one. It’s all about helping others (and the dining staff) know what’s working and what’s not.

2. Personalized Food Discovery
Looking for meals that match your dietary needs? No problem! Users can filter or tag meals and reviews by dietary preferences or allergies—like vegan, halal, gluten-free, making it easy to find food that fits you.

3. Flexible Review Formats
Everyone has their own way of expressing feedback, so we made it flexible. You can leave a review through a quick photo, a star/slider/number rating, or a written message—whatever feels right for you!

Mid-Fidelity Prototype

Interaction flow of the mid-fidelity protoype

1. Selecting either the camera capture button or the photo upload button will capture or upload an image and redirect the user to the “New Post” page for further edits and reviews.

2. The “New Post” page allows users to add various elements to their post, such as images, tags, a rating system, and captions. Clicking the Post button will publish the review to the feed.

3. On the Menu page, users can click the options button located at the top right corner of the title bar to access filter settings and adjust the menu view based on their preferences.

4. The navigation bar at the bottom enables users to switch between the corresponding pages.

How this mid-fidelity prototype addresses each of the final requirements:

My prototype allows the users to write and post detailed reviews with the “New Post” page. They can write captions about the food, add tags to narrow down their selection and also give it a star rating per the first requirement.

My prototype allows the users to be able to filter/tag dining hall meal options and reviews based on their food allergies and dietary preferences. This filter can be applied when posting on the “New Post” page, and can be used to narrow down search/options on the feed and the menu page.

My prototype also allows the users to make reviews in three optional ways. These are through images, text/star reviews, and these can all be seen in the “New Post” page. Here is the link to my Mid-Fidelity Prototype if you want to interact with it.

Carls Eats Figma App Prototype - Part 2

At this stage, I had a working prototype that addressed key dining hall issues at Carleton College, as shown in Part 1. To refine it further, my team developed a UX evaluation protocol and conducted another round of student interviews using our mid-fidelity prototypes. In this section, I’ll outline the criteria we used and how we incorporated feedback to improve and finalize our high-fidelity prototypes.

UX Evaluation Process

Method used: Focus Group

We conducted a focus group session to evaluate our mid-fidelity food rating app prototype. Two participants were guided through a series of tasks while completing given tasks, allowing us to observe their interactions and gather valuable feedback in real time. This method helped us understand how users interpreted design elements, navigated the interface, and identified usability issues. Group discussion also encouraged users to reflect on their experience and suggest improvements collaboratively.

Participants:

  • Sophomore with 15 meal plan – Currently eats at the dining hall and has some reasons for switching meal plans.
  • Senior with offboard meal plan– Has experienced all types of meal plans throughout college and currently is not on the meal plan.

Though I only interviewed these two stakeholders, I had enough different perspectives which helped me evaluate the usability of the app from a student-centered lens. While we focused on student feedback during this round, we recognize the value of incorporating dining staff perspectives in future evaluations.

UX evaluation tasks

  • Task 1:Viewing and interacting with food reviews

    Scenario: A user wants to check others’ opinions about current dining hall meals and explore those reviews.

  • Task 2: Saving a post based on food preferences:

    Scenario: A user spots a post that matches their dietary needs and wants to save it for future reference.

  • Task 3: Creating a food review post

    Scenario: A user wants to share their positive dining experience by posting an image and review of their meal.

UX metrics used

  • Clarifying Questions: Tracked the number of clarifying questions users asked while completing each task to assess how intuitive the interface was for Task 1.
  • Error Rate: Counted the number of misclicks or unintended interactions with prototype elements during task execution for Task 2.
  • Task Completion Time:Measured how long it took users to hypothetically capture, create, and post a food review using a timer from start to finish for Task 3.

UX Evaluation Results

* Overall User Experience

Easy to Navigate: Both participants found the app intuitive and straightforward to use. Tasks were completed with minimal confusion, indicating that my prototype has a strong foundational UX design.

Participant 1: "The tasks were pretty easy… I feel like it’s easy to navigate, like the design and the features."

Recognizable Icons: The Save button (bookmark icon) was easily identifiable and consistent with other apps, which helped users understand its function quickly.

Participant 2: "That Save button is pretty common in a lot of social media apps, so I think it was a good choice."

* Metrics collected

Task 1: Average number of clarifying questions asked during task

  • Participant 1 - 0
  • Participant 2 - 1

Task 2: Number of wrong errors committed during task

  • Participant 1 - 0
  • Participant 2 - 2

Task 3: Time taken to capture food during task

  • Participant 1 - 21.37 secs
  • Participant 2 - 34.17 secs
* Participant Suggestions

Bigger Camera: Participant 2 suggested a bigger camera to cover larger amounts of the screen to mimic an actual camera.

Privacy Option: Participant 1 suggested an option to stay anonymous per user's preference. The Me page should have an option to hide a users name and profile picture when making posts on the feed for confidentiality purposes

Carls Eats Figma App Prototype - Final Part

In this final part, I use all the feedback I received from the UX evaluation, in addition to the initial interviews in part 1, to design a high-fidelity version of my Carls Eats prototype.

High-Fidelity Prototype

Improvements based on UX Evaluation

1. Bigger Camera

Improvement: Fit the camera size to the sides of the screen for more range and familiarity to an actual camera

2. Privacy Option

Improvement: Included a privacy button in the Me tab so that users can specify how they want their identity to be shared on the platform

3. Save Button

Improvement: Save button highlights when clicked on a post and can be accessed in the Saved Posts page when navigated to in the Me page.

4. Intuitive Feed

Improvement: Added a profile picture and student name at the top of each post as a regular feed has. The comments, save button and tags were all moved to the bottom of each post to signify a caption.

5. More interactivity

Improvement: Added animations for filter toggle in the Menu page and saved button toggle in the Home page.

5. Improved user-friendliness

Improvement: Added ratings in the Menu page to show how well the dining hall menu is doing that day. Added common emojis in the Me page to make the prototype more welcoming and intuitive.

Here is the link to my High-Fidelity Prototype

Future steps

1. Add More Post Interactivity (e.g., Comment Threads, Reactions, Upvotes): Adding a Reddit-like comments feature where users could write detailed reviews or even create discussion threads will add richer interactivity and would allow for deeper conversations around food quality, recommendations, and dining hacks, enhancing user engagement. This will be implemented both in the Figma prototype and the app itself when it developed.

2. Add a Search or Filter Feature to Retrieve Lost Posts Both participants noted that if a post was lost or not saved in time, there should be a quicker way to find it — either via scrolling, search, or filter options.

Participant 1: "Hopefully there could be like a search bar, and I could search up whatever food I was thinking about."

3. Improve Visual Design Consistency (Image Layout, Same text): A cleaner, more consistent layout would enhance user comfort and credibility of the app.
